Lessons from Sam Altman: On Belief, Ambition, and High-Impact Careers

March 13, 2023 ¬∑ 7 minute read

Sam Altman is one of the most influential figures in technology. He's the CEO of OpenAI, former president of Y Combinator, and an early-stage investor in companies like Stripe, Reddit, and Airbnb.

He's also one of the most interesting thinkers of our time. His blog, which he's maintained since 2009, is a treasure trove of insights on technology, startups, and career development.

I've spent the past few weeks reading through his blog posts, looking for patterns and insights that could help me in my own career. Here's what I found.

On Belief

Sam believes that the most successful people are those who can maintain strong beliefs in the face of doubt. He writes:

"The most successful people I know believe in themselves almost to the point of delusion... Cultivate this delusion. As you get more data points that your judgment is good and you can consistently deliver results, trust yourself more."

This isn't blind faith. It's a carefully cultivated belief in your own judgment, backed by evidence and experience. The key is to start small, build a track record of success, and gradually increase your level of conviction.

But belief alone isn't enough. You also need to be right about important things. Sam emphasizes the importance of being "right about the future":

"The most successful people I know are primarily right about the future... If you can develop a reputation for being right about important things, people will come to trust your judgment."

On Ambition

Sam is known for his ambitious goals. He wants to create artificial general intelligence, extend human lifespan, and make humanity a multi-planetary species. But he didn't start with these moonshot goals. He built up to them gradually:

"The best way to become more ambitious is to become more ambitious about slightly bigger things one at a time. The most successful people I know did not start out wanting to change the world... They started with small ambitions and kept upgrading them as they achieved their previous goals."

He also emphasizes the importance of being ambitious about the right things:

"It's important to be ambitious about things that matter. Many people are ambitious about things that don't matter much in the long run... The best way to have impact is to work on things that will still matter in 10 or 20 years."

On Career Development

Sam has strong views on career development, particularly for young people. He advises against optimizing for optionality:

"The most common mistake I see young people make is to optimize for optionality... They end up with a lot of options but no real skills or expertise. It's better to pick something and go all in."

Instead, he recommends focusing on developing specific skills and expertise:

"The best career advice I can give is to do things that compound. Learn skills that will be valuable for decades. Build relationships with talented people. Create things that continue to create value while you sleep."

On Work and Life

While Sam is known for his intense work ethic, he also emphasizes the importance of sustainable productivity:

"Working hard is important, but working on the right things is more important... The best work requires periods of deep focus followed by periods of rest and reflection."

He also stresses the importance of taking care of yourself:

"Your productivity is a function of your health, both physical and mental. Exercise, sleep, and meditation are not indulgences - they're investments in your ability to do good work."

Key Takeaways

1. Build strong beliefs based on evidence and experience.

2. Be ambitious, but start small and gradually increase your goals.

3. Focus on developing specific skills rather than maintaining optionality.

4. Work on things that compound and matter in the long run.

5. Take care of your health to maintain sustainable productivity.

Sam's insights have helped shape my thinking about career development and personal growth. His emphasis on belief, ambition, and long-term thinking provides a valuable framework for making career decisions.

The key is to start small, build evidence that supports your judgment, and gradually take on bigger challenges. Focus on developing specific skills rather than maintaining optionality, and always prioritize work that compounds over time.

Most importantly, remember that success is not just about working hard - it's about working on the right things and taking care of yourself in the process.
